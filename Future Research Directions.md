# Future Research Directions

Paths forward for MPM in planetary impact science, as outlined in Section 5.2.

### 1. Rubble-Pile Impact Studies
- Model shock propagation through rubble-pile asteroids
- Compare with fractured monolith response
- Contact forces between boulders via grid-based contact
- Natural application of MPM's discontinuity handling

### 2. Subsurface Structure Effects
- How does a deep regolith layer alter impact outcomes?
- Effect of large subsurface voids on shock propagation
- Impact response of layered asteroids (differentiated interiors)
- MPM uniquely suited to model these complexities

### 3. MPM-DEM Coupling
- Seamless transition from MPM fragmentation to DEM gravitational reaccumulation
- Passing accurate fragment shapes (not just masses/velocities)
- Combined simulation of complete collisional process
- More realistic than current SPH-DEM or FDEM approaches

### 4. Material Model Expansion
- Extend validated model library beyond basalt
- Include metals, ices, carbonaceous materials
- Broader investigation of diverse collisional histories

### 5. Numerical Method Improvements
- Explore SGMP (Staggered Grid Material Point) for enhanced stability (Liang et al., 2019)
- Address GIMP over-suppression effects
- Parallelization for larger-scale simulations

### 6. Multi-Code Collaborative Studies
- Integrate MPM with other benchmarked shock physics codes
- Collaborative simulation of collision problems
- More comprehensive understanding through method diversity

### 7. Binary Asteroid Formation
- Study formation mechanisms of binary asteroid systems
- Role of impact in creating satellites

### 8. Planetary Defense Applications
- Improved predictions for kinetic impactor missions
- Modeling DART-like scenarios with realistic target interiors
- Momentum enhancement (β factor) studies
