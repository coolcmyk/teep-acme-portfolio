# Eros-Like Remnant

The discovery that MPM simulations can produce large, coherent asteroid fragments analogous to (433) Eros (Section 4.2, Fig. 14).

### The Fragment (Case b2)
- Formed under: Modified Lundborg strength model + high damage strength (~40 MPa)
- Shape: prolate (elongated)
- Maximum dimension: 16 km
- Complex 3D topology with significant concavities

### Comparison with (433) Eros
| Property | Simulated Fragment | (433) Eros |
|----------|-------------------|------------|
| Max dimension | 16 km | 34.4 km |
| Effective diameter | — | 16.84 km |
| Triaxial shape | Prolate | 34.4 × 11.2 × 11.2 km |
| Type | S-type basalt | S-type |
| Origin | Single impact on parent | Debate: monolith vs rubble pile |

### Scientific Significance

**The Eros Debate**:
- Richardson et al.: Eros is a "fractured monolith" — large shard from a parent body
- Tonge et al. (2016): strong internal flaw distribution needed to reproduce Eros surface features
- Ballouz et al. (2025): seismic data suggests rubble-pile-like interior

**What This Paper Adds**:
- Traditional SPH simulations struggle to produce large monolithic fragments — typically result in smaller debris that reaccumulates
- MPM b2 simulation is the FIRST numerical demonstration that a single catastrophic impact on a strong parent body can directly produce an Eros-sized and shaped shattered monolith
- A heavily shattered but not disrupted body could exhibit seismic properties similar to a rubble pile
- Bridges the gap between fractured monolith and rubble pile hypotheses

### Implications
- Some SPH models may overestimate damage in asteroid disruption events
- First strong numerical evidence for Eros as a giant shard from a primordial parent
